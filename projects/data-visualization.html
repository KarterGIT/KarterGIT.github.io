The capstone

DataScope Analytics — turning the AI job market into a decision system.

For the course capstone I played the role of a workforce-intelligence consultancy advising a tech company's executive team — the CPO, VP of Talent, and CFO. Starting from a 10,345-posting AI & Data Science job market dataset (2020–2026), I built an end-to-end Tableau decision system: a two-dashboard story with four KPI tiles that update live with every filter.

The design rule: every chart answers a decision the Board actually has to make — not just a metric to look at. Underneath it sits a full semester of weekly Tableau builds: maps, filters, calculated fields, and dashboard design fundamentals.

How it was built

From raw rows to executive answers

Three board-level questions

The whole dashboard hangs on three strategic questions: which roles are emerging vs. fading, where salary risk concentrates, and which skills carry a real premium. Every visualization ties back to one of them.

Data quality, checked first

Before any chart: the full dataset was audited against a validity / consistency / accuracy framework — types, ranges, duplicates, missing values, and outliers. Salary extremes were kept deliberately: they're the ceiling the Board benchmarks against.

Custom calculated fields

Built the analysis layer the raw schema couldn't answer: DATEPARSE time-series dates, a skill-count measure, five conditional skill-salary fields, and a dynamic Top Skill KPI that re-computes as filters change.

Finding: a $40K salary gap

Senior AI/ML Engineers earn ~$165K against ~$125K for other senior roles — a $40K exposure gap. Each experience level adds a steady ~$24K, while country differences are noise (~$3K). Role and experience are the real levers.

Finding: skills that actually pay

Deep Learning ($121K), Machine Learning ($121K), and Cloud ($118K) carry a real premium — while Python and SQL are table stakes: expected of everyone, worth no bump on their own. A clean, actionable ranking for job descriptions and training budgets.

Finding: where hiring runs hottest

A country × role urgency heatmap surfaced three hotspots — USA × Business Analyst (148), Singapore × ML Engineer (143), Australia × Business Analyst (142) — the exact cells where recruiting capacity should go first.

Chart craft — choosing the honest visual

Two design calls defined the project. A stacked bar chart quietly summed averages into a fake $420K figure — caught it, and rebuilt as side-by-side bars that preserve true values. And a world map with only 7 countries told no story, so it became a heatmap that shows country and role at the same time. Every recommendation shipped as if-this-then-who-does-what-by-when — a report an executive can act on, not just read.

Why it matters

Good dashboards answer decisions, not just show data.

This project is where I learned to work a dataset end to end — audit it, build the calculated layer, choose honest visuals, and land on recommendations someone can act on. It's also a study of the exact market I'm heading into: the AI job market itself.
